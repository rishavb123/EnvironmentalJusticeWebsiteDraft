About Environmental Justice

Georgia Tech is home to one of the most diverse student populations in the entire state of Georgia. Yellow Jackets come from all over the world, and our common purpose to create the future is what unites us. Just like how Georgia Tech is home to us, Atlanta is home to Georgia Tech. Members of the Atlanta community, predominantly poor African American families, suffer from the injustices of inequitable environmental protection and regulation. As Yellow Jackets, if we are to build the future, it starts with building up the community around us by seeking environmental justice.

Humble Beginnings

The Environmental Justice movement was born out of the Civil Rights movement when Martin Luther King, Jr. lent his aid to the Sanitation Strike of 1968. The night before MLK’s assassination, he bravely told a group of sanitation workers that “we’ve got to give ourselves to this struggle until the end.” Those fateful words would mark the beginning of a historic movement to ensure equity for all people in terms of environmental protection. The year 2018 marked the 50th anniversary of the Environmental Justice movement, and though a lifetime of progress has been made to bring about environmental justice, we must remember that there is still a lot of work left to do.

Why It Matters?

Environmental injustices in Atlanta should matter to Georgia Tech students for several reasons. Perhaps the most prominent reason is that we all live near the heart of Atlanta; whatever happens to the city, happens to us. It is important that GT students care about their immediate surroundings. Looking at the maps on this page dealing with tree cover, income, and environmental status, there is a clear correlation between these factors. The mission statement of Georgia Tech includes making a meaningful contribution to society, including our immediate surroundings. The overlap of the surrounding graphs makes it clear that certain areas of Atlanta are underserved and suffering from poor environmental conditions. Students should care because these people are suffering all around us and as students of Georgia Tech, it is our duty to do whatever we can to help those suffering.

Environmental Injustice in Atlanta

Currently, one of the largest environmental justice issues facing Atlanta is air pollution. Impoverished communities tend to live closer to sources of air pollution. While it is a highly dangerous problem, people have been stepping up in recent years with new ideas to combat this issue. Click the image to the left for more information.
Air Pollution

Air pollution is a large issue for many citizens around Atlanta and throughout all of Georgia that live below the poverty line. Toxic gases from coal-fired power plants are produced all over EPA region 4, which includes Georgia. African American communities suffer disproportionately from this issue due to their proximity to sources of pollution. In 2011, the EPA proposed a plan to cut down on toxic emissions from coal-powered plants across the state. It is estimated that African American communities in Georgia are 30% more likely to be affected by a coal plant’s toxins than any other community. It was estimated that this plan would save 17,000 lives and prevent over 100,000 cases of asthma. The expected value of these improvements was around $100 billion a year. To put this in perspective, this article states that for every dollar spent on this beneficial project, the average american citizen would see an increase of around $10 in health benefits. This movement has been extraordinarily helpful to Georgians and people living in the Atlanta area since Georgia is one of the top 5 states in number of coal-based power plants near low income communities. New limitations on power plant location have also been established. New factories must be built a certain distance away from any community to safeguard their health. This event can be seen as a great win for environmental justice. Because the EPA brought this problem to the center stage, new laws have been created to help protect the right of every person to equal health.

Atlanta faces issues in abandoned housing and poorly kept properties as a result of inequity and improper waste management. Malpractice in waste disposal and a lack of infrastructure to support waste management are key sources of urban dereliction in Atlanta. Click the image to the right for more information.
Abandoned Housing

Urban dereliction is a major problem within the Atlanta community. It is the result of unequally distributed wealth and poor environmental standards which cause properties to become unwanted and abandoned. This in turn lowers the property value of the communities and members within the communities are forced to live with uncollected trash and other hazardous waste. These issues emanate from discrimination in policy making in regards to environmental standards. There is a clear difference in the amount of trees and other plants in areas that have more white people than areas with more people of color. That is, there are more trees in places with more white residences. With pollutants, the opposite is true where we see people of color are in the same areas where dumps and waste facilities are located. It is evident that one of the key issues is the amount of litter and illegal dumps found in these communities as 71% of surveyed people living in neighborhoods with high dereliction said that the trash was a problem for their community. The same survey was done with people living in areas with low levels of dereliction and only 20% reported trash as an issue for their community. From this, there is a connection between how waste is managed in comparison the demographics of the area, which shows the ongoing environmental injustice occurring in Atlanta.

There are rivers around Atlanta which are still impacted by the racist environmental practices from the 20th century. These rivers tend to flow through black communities and pollute these communities with toxic waters. Click the image to the left for more information.
Polluted Rivers

In Atlanta, environmental injustices from the 1980s are still plaguing the community, and the rivers around it are living proof of that. In 2010, the river at Panola Shoals was deemed unfit for people to swim in due to the amount of pollution in the river. It was found to be full of sewage waste and trash which puts communities around it at risk of dangerous diseases. This river was used as an outlet for sewage and other hazardous wastes for facilities from the mid 1900s. This same river also flows through many majority black communities and posed even more of a risk then before reforms were put into place to reduce the pollution going into it. Nevertheless, the origins of this river’s drastically low health standards are the result of racial discrimination and a lack of efficacy in the political decision making which created these waste paths. When the EPA became involved in 1998, Atlanta was required to fix the overflows of sewage and trash created by the river costing over $4 billion dollars. In the decree, however, only $25 million dollars needed to be spent on communities with larger populations of minorities and it simply stated that there must be “particular emphasis... placed on input from minority communities adjacent to the designated streams and other streams affected by the discharges from the Defendant’s CSO control facilities.” This decree in practice did very little to rectify the issues of environmental injustice seen in the Atlanta rivers and the river’s current state is proof of that. It is clear that waste management and race are connected and minorities are typically used as scapegoats for the pollution placed in their communities.
